Analysis: CPS could save close to $200 million by closing empty schools

Schools

By LocalLabs News Service | May 25, 2017


CPS keeps Fenger High School on Chicago's far South Side open, even though it has 271 students. The building once housed 5,300. | Wikipedia

Chicago Public Schools could save close to $200 million per year by closing more than 100 mostly empty schools it continues to operate and maintain.

Local Government Information Services (LGIS), which publishes Chicago City Wire, analyzed CPS’ published school occupancy data and found that, by the district’s own admission, 304 of the 566 buildings it operates are “underutilized,” or at least 20 percent empty.

 Of the underutilized schools, 116 are more than 50 percent empty; 77 of those are more than 70 percent empty.

 Closing all schools that are less than 50 percent full, and moving their 41,825 students elsewhere, would save at least $181 million, according to the LGIS analysis.

The savings would come from eliminating 1,448 duplicative teachers, administrative and operational staff, like custodians, lunchroom attendants, and security guards, at CPS’ most underutilized schools, according to LGIS.

It would include $123,429,354 in salary and benefits for those staff members, before their pension contributions and another $58 million in various school operating expenses, or an estimated average of $500,000 per year, per school.

If it made the cuts, CPS would be left with 446 schools that have a capacity of 352,173, serving 347,028 students. 

Over the past 15 years, CPS has spent $6.6 billion more than it has received in tax revenue.

In 2016, it spent $6.1 billion and received $5.2 billion, a deficit of $900 million.

Rather than close schools, CPS has continued building new ones. Most recently, a published report suggested the district is building a new high school in the South Loop.

The South Loop's current high school is Phillips, 244 E. Pershing Road, which has 635 students and a capacity of 1,834.
